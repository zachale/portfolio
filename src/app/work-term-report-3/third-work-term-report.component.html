Fall 2024 @ ApplyBoard
  I spent my third work term as a software engineering intern at ApplyBoard. This was an exciting opportunity to work with a different company and I continued to be an independent contributor as part of their engineering team. I developed full-stack solutions to resolve client feedback, realize new product features, and fix critical bugs.
  ApplyBoard builds a platform that simplifies the study abroad search, application, and acceptance process by connecting international students, recruitment partners, and academic institutions on one intuitive platform. Their customers include students from around the world and educational institutions. Their application has proven results helping students achieve their educational goals and facilitating connections between students and institutions through ApplyBoard's various solutions.
The ApplyBoard team including Zach
I was contributing to ApplyBoard's platform for the duration of my internship. As a software engineering intern, I was tasked with building and maintaining full-stack features based on specifications and fixing critical bugs. These specifications could arise from direct customer or user feedback, a UI/UX designer, or a myriad of other stakeholders. To build these features I utilized modern web technologies which included technologies like Typescript, Angular, Express.Js, Jest, Node and MongoDB. I built robust, impactful features and solutions that accrued thousands of user impressions and fulfilled specifications.
The most impactful project I developed in my third term was a critical race condition fix within their on-demand translation pipeline. After asking questions and understanding the root issue, I discovered that the given proposed solution was not scalable and would add unnecessary complexity. To mediate this, I advocated for and created a second solution that resolved the race condition directly. Because of my vocal advocacy, my solution was chosen and eventually proven most effective. One requirement for the solution was that functionality had to be maintained in highly concurrent and distributed situations. I validated this by completing several pair-testing sessions to trigger concurrent cloud functions. This project was an incredible learning opportunity and taught me the importance of advocating for my ideas. It also resulted in an estimated 90% reduction in peak on-demand translation costs.
My third term at ApplyBoard was another unforgettable experience. The technical and professional skills I developed will prove invaluable as I continue my career in software engineering. I look forward to what the future has in store, and I am excited to apply my new skills in my next co-op term.

Learning Goals
Development Planning
After completing my second co-op term, I received feedback that I needed to work on my planning ability. My previous term at 10KC was the first time my technical planning skills came under stress so receiving that criticism was incredibly valuable and allowed me to make it a focus of my third term. I achieved this goal by being very purposeful with ticket scoping, ensuring that I understood the impact of each ticket. As someone who loves to work on the fly, it was a challenge to pause and fully map out each ticket before diving in. This was an effective strategy, and I feel much more confident in my planning ability. By using feedback as fuel, I developed my technical planning skills to the next level.
Testing
Another area I wanted to improve my skills in this term was my testing ability. After reflecting on my previous terms, I realized that there were a handful of instances where anticipating the true impact of a small change was a challenge. For example, I wrote code for a small feature that was fully functional but missing a single character. Missing that one character caused a critical page to crash on production. This incident made it clear to me that I needed to have a full understanding of any side effects caused by my changes. I aimed to focus on reading testing files before changing functionality and ensuring at least a 90% test coverage on all new functionality created. I dramatically achieved this goal and wrote high-coverage testing for almost every single feature I built in my third term. Building such high-coverage testing unearthed a lot of challenges that were super fun to solve, independently or as part of a pair-debugging session. Even though I made a large mistake, I used that as a learning experience and pushed myself to walk away as a better developer.
Code Analysis
Something I found incredibly difficult was the transition from reading and analyzing code at school versus in industry. At school, I was given maybe a 20-line C snippet and asked to trace and debug it. I understood that working in the industry would require analyzing and reviewing more code, but what surprised me was the sheer volume and breadth of code. I found it quite challenging to digest relatively large pull requests where I lacked all context. One strategy I used was to set up a consistent PR review cadence. By getting consistent exposure to different code from across the stack, I gained more valuable code base context and improved my PR digesting skills. Another strategy I had was to set an exact number of PR reviews to complete before the end of the term. This gave me a target to hit and lit a fire under me to get to work. In reflection, this strategy proved ineffective for me. Aiming for only an exact quantity of reviews set incorrect incentives and ambiguous expectations. In the end, I fell short of the target, but I drastically improved my code analysis skills. I feel much more confident digesting and analyzing large pull requests and understanding context before I dive in.
