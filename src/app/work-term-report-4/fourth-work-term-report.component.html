Winter 2026 @ RBC
  I spent my fourth work term as a GenAI Engineer intern at the Royal Bank of Canada. This was my first time working in finance and my first time building production AI systems. I designed, built, and shipped LLM-powered tools for RBC's Internal Audit team.
  RBC is Canada's largest bank, serving millions of clients across personal and commercial banking, wealth management, and capital markets. I worked on the IDNA team (Innovation, Data, and Analytics) within Internal Audit, an enablement group focused on building tools that help auditors work more efficiently. I was one of two interns on the team, working alongside full-time engineers, data scientists, and business stakeholders.
Robert's team, including Zach (left)
I worked solo on most of my projects this term, with strong support from senior engineers and data scientists on the team. As a GenAI Engineer intern, I was tasked with designing and building LLM-based tools to make auditors' work faster. To build these tools I used Python, LangChain, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Vue, Nuxt, TypeScript, and MySQL. I built robust, impactful systems that turned manual, multi-hour tasks into workflows that take minutes.
The most impactful project I built this term was the Process Mapper, a tool that converts SOP documents and meeting transcripts into editable, visual process maps. The pipeline is built on a two-phase LangChain architecture: the first phase extracts a structured SOP from the raw input, capturing steps, owners, systems, and risks; the second phase converts that structured representation into constrained Mermaid flowchart code, which is rendered in the browser through a custom SVG editor that lets auditors click, pan, zoom, and edit the diagram directly. This collapsed a process that had previously taken roughly six hours per diagram down to about two minutes. The tool was originally scoped for the ~700 auditors in Internal Audit, but it was selected over four other internal solutions to become the future enterprise standard for process mapping at RBC, with a path toward 100,000+ employees enterprise-wide. Demoing this product to more than twenty audiences across the bank, including senior leaders, was a huge boost to my communication skills this term.
My term at RBC was an unforgettable experience. Shipping a tool that scaled to the enterprise level as an intern was a high I do not expect to forget, and the skills I picked up will prove invaluable as I continue my career. I am especially excited to keep building agentic AI systems in whatever I work on next.

Learning Goals
Building Agentic Systems
Going into this term, I wanted to become more proactive at identifying and pursuing agentic solutions to problems. My plan was to volunteer for projects involving automation or AI-driven workflows and to contribute to at least three agentic modules over the course of the term. To be honest, I did not achieve this goal in the way I expected. Instead of building agents, I built linear LLM systems for transforming inputs into outputs. The Process Mapper is the clearest example, a deterministic two-phase chain rather than an agentic loop. I am a little disappointed I did not get the chance to build something fully autonomous, but I picked up great experience designing and shipping production LLM systems either way.
Oral Communication
I came into this term wanting to grow more comfortable presenting my work to a variety of audiences. I planned to attend a biweekly public speaking practice event and to seek out opportunities to demo my work to stakeholders at varying levels of seniority. I achieved this goal tenfold. The Process Mapper grew larger than my team had anticipated, and I ended up demoing it to more than twenty different audiences across the bank and receiving feedback from all of them. I was also invited to a Toastmasters club run by someone on the larger team, and joined in to practice speaking under pressure. At the end of the term, my manager gave me a great review on my improved public speaking and presenting skills, and specifically highlighted my growth over the four months. Of my three goals this term, this is the one I am proudest of.
Python Proficiency
I also wanted to deepen my proficiency with Python by prioritizing Python-first approaches and contributing to at least three Python pull requests over the term. Unfortunately, I did not get to work with Python as much as I had hoped. I contributed to some R&D work in Python, and the Process Mapper backend itself is a Python and LangChain codebase, but to be honest I leaned on AI tooling so much that I wrote relatively little code by hand. Most of what I shipped ended up being TypeScript on the frontend. I did work with Python this term, just not as much as I was hoping for, and I did not push any Python pull requests.
