Winter 2026 @ RBC
  I spent my fourth work term as a GenAI Engineer intern at the Royal Bank of Canada. This was my first time working in finance and my first time building production AI systems end to end. I designed, built, and shipped LLM-powered tools for RBC's Internal Audit team.
  RBC is Canada's largest bank, serving millions of clients across personal and commercial banking, wealth management, and capital markets. I worked on the IDNA team (Innovation, Data, and Analytics) within Internal Audit, an enablement group focused on building tools that help auditors work more efficiently. I was one of two interns on the team, working alongside full-time engineers, data scientists, and business stakeholders.
The IDNA team, including Zach (left)
I worked solo on most of my projects this term, with strong support from senior engineers and AI/ML specialists across the team. As a GenAI Engineer intern, I was tasked with identifying opportunities to apply LLMs within the audit workflow, then designing and delivering tools to capture them. The stack I worked with included Python, LangChain, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Vue, Nuxt, TypeScript, and MySQL. I built robust, impactful systems that turned manual, multi-hour tasks into workflows that take minutes.
The flagship project I built this term was the Process Mapper, a tool that converts SOP documents and meeting transcripts into editable, visual process maps. The pipeline is built on a two-phase LangChain architecture: the first phase extracts a structured SOP from the raw input, capturing steps, owners, systems, and risks; the second phase converts that structured representation into constrained Mermaid flowchart code, which is rendered in the browser through a custom SVG editor that lets auditors click, pan, zoom, and edit the diagram directly. This collapsed a process that had previously taken roughly six hours per diagram down to about two minutes. The tool was originally scoped for the ~700 auditors in Internal Audit, but it was selected over four other internal solutions to become the future enterprise standard for process mapping at RBC, with a path toward 100,000+ employees across the bank. Demoing this product to more than twenty different audiences, ranging from individual contributors to senior leaders, taught me more about communicating technical work than any course or talk ever could.
My term at RBC was a defining experience. Shipping an AI product at enterprise scale as an intern showed me what is possible when strong technical execution meets the right opportunity, and it set the bar for the kind of impact I want to keep chasing. I am excited to keep building agentic and AI-powered systems, and to bring the lessons from this term into whatever comes next.

Learning Goals
Building Agentic Systems
Going into this term, I wanted to grow as an engineer who proactively identifies and pursues agentic solutions to problems. My plan was to volunteer for projects involving automation or AI-driven workflows and to contribute to at least three agentic modules over the course of the term. To be honest, I did not achieve this goal in the way I expected. Instead of building autonomous agents, I built linear LLM systems that transformed inputs into outputs through carefully designed pipelines. The Process Mapper is the clearest example of this, a deterministic two-phase chain rather than an agentic loop. I am a little disappointed that I did not get the chance to build something fully autonomous, but the experience I gained designing, evaluating, and shipping production LLM systems was invaluable, and it has given me a strong foundation to build agentic solutions on top of in the future.
Oral Communication
I came into this term wanting to grow more comfortable presenting my work to a variety of audiences. I planned to attend a biweekly public speaking practice event and to actively seek out opportunities to demo my work to stakeholders at varying levels of seniority. I achieved this goal tenfold. The Process Mapper grew larger than I or my team had anticipated, and I ended up demoing it to more than twenty different audiences, ranging from individual contributors to senior leadership across multiple business lines. I also took part in a Toastmasters club run on the larger team, which gave me a consistent space to practice speaking under pressure and receive feedback. At the end of the term, my manager highlighted my improved public speaking and presenting skills in his evaluation, and specifically called out the growth in my communication over the four months. This is the area I am most proud of growing in this term.
Python Proficiency
I also wanted to deepen my proficiency with Python by prioritizing Python-first approaches and contributing to at least three Python pull requests over the term. Unfortunately, I did not get to work with Python as much as I had hoped. I did contribute to some R&D work in Python, and the Process Mapper backend itself is a Python and LangChain codebase, but I leaned heavily on AI tooling throughout the term and ended up writing relatively little code by hand. The majority of the code I shipped was in TypeScript on the frontend rather than Python on the backend. While I did not hit my pull request target, I came away with a much better sense of how to use AI tooling effectively in a Python codebase, which is a skill I expect to keep refining.
